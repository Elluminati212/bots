Now, you might be wondering, why did a single assassination lead to a global conflict? The answer lies in the complex web of alliances between European powers. Imagine a giant game of chess, where each country was trying to outmaneuver the others. The Triple Entente, consisting of France, Britain, and Russia, faced off against the Central Powers, made up of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy. The war itself was brutal and devastating. Trench warfare, where soldiers on both sides dug in for years, became the norm. The introduction of new technologies like machine guns, tanks, and poison gas made the conflict even more deadly. The war of attrition, where both sides suffered heavy losses, led to a stalemate that lasted for years. The human cost was staggering: over 37 million casualties, including 11 million military deaths.

**Segment 4: The Turning Point and the Aftermath**

The war finally turned in favor of the Allies with the entry of the United States in 1917. The American troops brought fresh manpower and resources, which helped to break the stalemate. The Armistice of November 11, 1918, marked the end of the war, but the aftermath was just as significant. The Treaty of Versailles imposed harsh penalties on Germany, which many believe contributed to the rise of Nazi Germany and the outbreak of World War II.
